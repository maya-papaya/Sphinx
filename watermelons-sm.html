Down By The Bay Where The Watermelons Grow…
By: Sadie Grey Murphy

“Somewhere on Cape Cod”

Some place small there’s a stand alone house.
It’s painted with sweat and the sound of dragonflies swimming in the sun.
It faces away from the people with their hearts, and towards the sea.
It’s chilly there, like a japanese summer in july.
Time passes strangely as a cat whispers on the front doorstep.
Visitors come as frequently as the seasons.

“The Uninvited Guest”

There’s a room kept separate from the rest of the house.
The light there refracts at an angle that makes the shadows dance.
The walls are painted the color of a sigh that vanishes into the huckleberry floor.
On a table, in the back corner (a little left of center) stands a swan.
Her poise is known to the portrait, whose eyes follow her and her felicity.
Air floats in from the hole in the ceiling and reminds her she must take flight.
And when she leaves, the mice wedged under the floorboard miss her.

“The French Sailor”

A little boy lives here during the summers and he wishes that meant forever.
He daydreams in the itchy tall grass next to the meadowlark’s nest.
He watches the boats go by and thinks that someday that will be him.
The water sparkles as he dips his toes into the edge of its promise.
He chases the wind for a hat that’s printed with the life he’ll have ahead of him.
It's full of his hopes and his smiles and it costs him his happiness.

“The Shy Sailor”

As he grows older, the boy covers his face with his skin.
He’s got a small model boat that accompanies him and his fears.
They travel together like water and a waning moon.
Their background is the sea and the waves which surround them.
You can hear their calls from the inside of a tulip shell if you listen closely.

“The Race”

One day he’s a boy and the next he’s a man.
He’s still young and new in the world and he finds comfort in the water.
He likes being swallowed by its vastness and wholeness.
Now, he’s got a list of friends that’s longer than the chain of an anchor.
They would go out to sea for lunch on the island tucked away from the eyes.
And one day their boat tipped over, sinking from the weight of their screams.
And the man was a was.

“She’s Gone”

Thousands upon thousands gathered near the edge of the water.
Sand covered up the dying grass a little beyond where he used to play.
They were strangers but friends, all knowing each other were knowing.
They brought smiles and hope and pieces of him.
They were his great grandmother or his fifth cousin.
They were his millions of hearts, facing where he had left them.
As maybes and laters and tiny grains of sand.
