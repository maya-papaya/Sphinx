The Idiot’s Guide to Grief
Maya Reddy

Grief is strange,
So I’ll walk you through it.

You’ll start by sobbing about a scraped knee,
The wound, a sickly shining red.
You’ll move on to tantrums,
Screaming for ice cream, toys, or even that pretty earring Mom always wore.
You’ll then advance:
Up to missing your grandparents,
Wailing at the airport as they disappear into the crowds.
And that will be it, if you’re lucky.

And then she’ll collapse on the floor.
And they’ll say it’s just a checkup.
But you know what’s going to happen,
Because it happens in the movies.
And you’ll see her frail scrawny body,
Wrapped in your bright pink robe with unicorns.
And you know Dad will never get to say goodbye,
And your heart aches at that thought.
And you’ll pull out of the parking lot,
Never forgetting the dark grey-blue sky and red shining stoplight.
And you’ll force tears at home when the deed is done,
Because you think there’s something wrong with you for not crying.
And the health center will give you cards and pamphlets to guide you,
And you’ll begin addressing letters to ‘Rainbow Bridge’,
Because that’s where they said she’ll go.
And the next day, probably at school,
You’ll meander into the bathroom and your shock dissipates,
So you’ll weep there for awhile, staring out of the window through blurry tears.
And you’ll play ‘Heart and Soul’ on the piano.
And you’ll spread her ashes at the bottom of a tree,
Only to find a year later that the tree is infested with disease
And is slowly dying.
And then you’ll question what grief is and what love is,
If you really feel anything.
And everyone else moves on and tries to wake you from this stupor,
Saying you need to stop.
And they’ll move her favorite couch to the basement,
Then donate her sparkly bed where you used to sit with her.
And eventually you’ll move on and wonder at
How fickle your feelings are.

And one day after all those storms have passed,
You’ll happen across the planks in front of the steps.
And you’ll see little paw prints scattered here and there,
And you know it’s her footsteps impressed in the varnish.
And you’ll be able to smile a little.
It’s a start.

You’re welcome.
This is just a warning, a meager description of all the messiness that awaits you.
I give this to you as a gift so you don’t feel so confused.
Because doubting yourself is the first step on that slippery slope.
Trust me,
I would know.
